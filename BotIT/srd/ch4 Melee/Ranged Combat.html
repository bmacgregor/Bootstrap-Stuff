Ranged Combat Overview

In Blade, ranged combat can include attacks from a thrown rock to a steel-pronged crossbow, and every conceivable thrown or launched missile in between. Attacks may sometimes occur as a single, sniper-like attack with no chance for the target to react, but more frequently the attack will be launched into a scene of melee, or will perhaps lead to a melee situation. Such attacks are rolled with dice from the character’s Archery Pool (AP), consisting of Aim + Missile Proficiency. For the latter case, ranged combat must be integrated with melee combat, in order to give archers and the like a place in the Order of Limelights. This spot in the Limelight sequence is determined exactly as if the archer was a melee combatant, with the same principles holding true for both participants.

That is to say that sometimes, when it is clear who is going first (for example when an encounter is initiated by a crossbow bolt from an ambush), the ref will establish the Order of Limelights by calling up players in their logical order. But, in those cases where the sequence is more random, players will be called up to play out their characters’ Limelights in ascending order of CG, as described in greater detail in the section on Limelight and the Combat Round earlier in this chapter.

Making a Ranged Attack

During his Limelight, a character intending to launch a missile attack can do so, provided he has the weapon at hand. There is no preparation, loading, or aiming times, as a Limelight is considered a span of time sufficient to accomplish a significant task – and this certainly includes firing a weapon, not merely preparing to fire it. As melee combatants move about during their own Limelights, it will usually not be readily apparent at exactly what distance they are from a missile combatant during the missile combatant’s Limelight, and also not whether there is a free line of sight between the two. The ref is thus called upon to decide these things based upon common sense, the players’ description of their characters’ actions, and also the scene’s dramatic requirements.

If the player of a missile combatant is not happy with the ref’s judgement, he can have his character move into a more advantageous firing position. Such movement to get an unobstructed line of sight, or merely closer to the target is accomplished via a Terrain Check made with dice from the character’s Archery Pool. The ref first announces the Challenge Level to get to closer range, or to achieve a free line of sight (rarely harder than Difficult, if even that), and the player then attempts to pass the Check using dice out of his Archery Pool, rolling any amount of dice desired.

Success does of course mean that the character achieves his objective, while failure signifies that he either must make the shot at longer range or has to contend with some cover obscuring the target, perhaps both – it never means that a shot is outright impossible. A Terrain Check can also be used when a missile combatant is charged by an enemy and must shoot him down before being engaged in melee combat. In such cases, the ref decides the Challenge Level based on how close the charging party currently is, and how ready the missile combatant is to fire.

Casting a spear that is ready in hand before one is engaged will almost never be harder than Average, while spanning and discharging a heavy crossbow may be as difficult as Legendary, provided the attacker isn’t very far off. If, in the situation described in the preceding paragraph, both opponents are important PCs or NPCs, the Terrain Check is an Opposed one. The charging combatant first declares how many dice out of his MP he is going to spend so to engage before he can be fired at, and the missile combatant then declares the number of dice from his Archery Pool he is going to spend. Depending on the ref’s appraisal of the situation (closeness of opponent vs. readiness of the missile weapon), one opponent will likely need a QoS of just 1 to be successful, whereas the other may well need a higher QoS.

Whether the situation is handled by an Opposed Check or not, victory on the part of the missile combatant will mean that he gets a shot in at practically point blank range. Failure does however signify that he will be engaged before he can fire and that the Combat Round immediately commences, with the missile combatant’s initial MP reduced by a number of dice equalling those spent from his Archery Pool on the Terrain Check.

If he was trying to throw a spear or similar weapon useful in close combat, he may opt to retain it in his hand, combat-ready, otherwise he is assumed to have realized in time that his attempt to shoot his opponent would not pan out, has dropped his missile weapon and barely managed to whip out a melee weapon – if he has one.

Range Increments

Range, like many aspects of Blade combat, is handled in a slightly abstract way. It depends first and foremost on the principle that the ultimate limitation to all range is human vision - it doesn’t matter one whit that a heavy crossbow may well be able to launch a bolt over 300 yards when one can’t clearly discern the target at such range. Even missile weapons with theoretical maximum ranges as long as some bows and crossbows have realistic practical ranges that are only a fraction of that maximum. Extreme ranges are for wide-shooting contests and battlefield area-fire, not for taking down individual targets.

Practical ranges vary from 2-100 meters, and are divided into Range Bands from Point Blank to Extreme. What actual distance each Range Band signifies is different for each type of ranged weapon. Weapons that are thrown in such a way that they tumble while in the air (knifes and hatchets) have the least range, while those that are cast to fly straight (spears and javelins) middling range, and finally those that are launched (arrows, bolts and sling stones) the greatest.

Each Range Band determines how difficult or easy it is to aim for a specific body part by governing how many dice from the Archery Pool must be spent for a single +/-1 modifier on Table 18. The Target Zone of a ranged attack is determined randomly by rolling 2D6 on Table 4.5. A missile combatant does however have some degree of control over the exact Target Zone by spending dice from his Archery Pool before rolling for the attack.

The number of dice needed to be spent for a single +/-1 move on Table 18 is listed in each range band found in Table 4.4. Also note that at Point Blank range, the missile combatant receives a free modifier of +/-2; if your target is right in front of you, one is not likely to aim either high or low. Apart from this free +/- 2, the cost for further desired modifiers is the same as Near range. This modifier does not have to be spent in full on Table 4.5; it can also be spent in part or not at all, depending on the player’s wishes.

Cover

Cover can complicate a ranged attack, and while some obstacles can be avoided by means of a Terrain Check, this is not possible in all cases – no maneuvering in the world will do away with the cover given by a breastwork to defenders manning a wall. Soft cover that merely obstructs vision but does not completely block it, like foliage or a moving crowd of people, does impart a negative modifier to the Archery Pool.

Depending on the ref’s judgement of how much the target is obstructed from clear view by soft cover, this modifier can range from –1 to –3 per line on Table 4.4. The possible modifiers are therefore for example –1, –2, or –3 at Point Blank range (line one on Table 4.4), and –3, –6, and –9 at Medium range. It can range from a mild –1 for a target at Point Blank range past a single person moving through the line of sight, to a whopping –15 for a target at Extreme range that is made almost invisible by the densest of foliage.

The rules for soft cover also apply if one wants to fire into an on-going melee and hit one combatant while avoiding the other, which is of course difficult due to the unpredictable movements of both. In such a case, the minimum penalty for soft cover (i.e. –1 per line on Table 4.4, effectively –1 to –5) is used – and a Botch on the attack roll should usually mean a hit on a person the missile combatant does not wish to hit.

If that happens, the attack roll is repeated. The original penalty dice for firing into a melee still apply for this roll, but neither penalty dice for soft cover, nor any dice possibly set aside for buying modifiers on Table 4.5 do. Any such penalty dice on the original roll are added to the new attack roll, and any hit resulting from this second roll applied to the new target. The exact attack location on the new target is determined randomly on Table 4.5, with no modifiers to this roll.

Example: Otho is throwing his dagger at Far range to aid his friend Galen in his melee against two opponents. Otho has an Archery Pool of 13, from which 4 dice are subtracted for firing into a melee at Far range, and a further 3 dice for buying a +/–1 modifier on Table 4.5. Otho Botches the attack roll with his remaining 6 dice, and has to reroll his attack, now with Galen as target. The 4 dice penalty for firing into a melee still applies, but the 3 dice traded in for a +/–1 modifier don’t; his Archery Pool is therefore 10.

Should the attack hit, the damage location will be determined randomly on Table 19, without the +/–1 modifier Otho had purchased for his original attack.

Hard cover that cannot be fired through represents a more extreme problem for a missile combatant. If it can’t be circumvented to get a clear shot, the attacker will just have to take his chances by investing heavily into modifiers on Table 4.5 and hope that this modifier is sufficient to hit a location that is not covered.

Defending against missile attacks is of course only possible against attacks that one is aware of. It is preferably done with a large shield, or alternatively be evading. Details on that process and its associated TNs can be found in the section on defending above.
