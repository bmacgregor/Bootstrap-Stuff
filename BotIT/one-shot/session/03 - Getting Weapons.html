The Hounds

You find the baggage-train at the rear of the convoy - a large wagon, not unlike the one you and your new companions escaped from, though probably better kept. A pack of hounds lounge around it, apparently not bothered by the snows. From your vantage point, it looks like there are ten of the beasts. Eight of them are unremarkable; average war-dogs that one might expect to be set on peasants, each well muscled and vicious. The last two, however, are massive. A pair of great, shaggy beasts that tower over the rest, resting together by the wagon's front wheel. Idling nearby is a lazy Church Knight, Cob.

Cob

It's up to the Players on how to proceed. If they have the Valerian (or what they think is the Valerian), now would be the time to mix it with the raw meat stolen from the hound master's saddlebags and throw it - hopefully from somewhere out of view - to the dogs. Dealing with Cob, however, is another matter entirely.

If a Player comes within view, Cob will absolutely shout a warning, unless they are disguised as Church Knights. Cob, himself is new to the group, but is extremely distrustful. The third son of a merchant who wanted to hedge his bets, Cob has been set to the Gray Church for two reasons - first, to get him out of the way (Cob is arrogant and lazy, but full of low cunning) and second, to hopefully win some good will from the Gray Church.

Cob is shirking his duties, avoiding the main effort of helping his brothers clear the road, or get the camp ready for the night's halt. Nevertheless, he's quite alert, and probably won't be snuck up on. If the Players intend to ambush and kill Cob, they will need to Check Sneaking, and need to roll three successes to take him unawares. If a Player fails this check, what happens next depends largely on how they appear. If they are dressed as Church Knights, Cob will jump up, and immediately pretend that he was minding the dogs - his hands groping for food he has concealed in his pockets, implying that he's been feeding them. He will greet the interlopers with a cheerful tone, but Players can roll Falsehood to see through this act, noting that he's clearly trying to hide his true intentions (in this case, simple laziness).

If the Player's aren't disguised when (and if) they're noticed, Cob won't immediately raise an alarm. Instead, he'll blurt out "Who the hell are you?!" in a sharp whisper. If the Players charge in to attack, Cob will raise his hands in a sign of peace or surrender, and beg the Characters to wait. The dogs, however, will definitely begin putting up a fuss, and in the event of combat, will absolutely attack.

Cob hates the Church Knights, and wants to desert - all he needs is a chance to do so. He's stolen a key to the baggage train, and intends to filch supplies from it before escaping into the forest and heading towards Whitelake, a nearby village. He doesn't know anything about the Cauldron (other than it gives him the shivers), and if the Characters agree to cause chaos to let him slip off, he's more than willing to open the wagon with his key for them.

This little scene will, naturally, require some Role Play on the part of the Players, but can be skipped entirely if Cob is somehow led away from the baggage. Perhaps an enterprising Player wants to attempt an Acting check, or some other plan. If they come up with something, absolutely let them try. It shouldn't require more than two sucesses, unless their idea is totally unfeasable. If they manage to lure Cob off, all they have to deal with are the dogs, which is described below.


The Dogs

If Cob is gotten rid of quietly, and so long as they're holding food, the dogs won't immediately attack - low growls will be heard, and the large hounds will turn to look at the Player. Feeding the dogs this way is extremely ill-advised. Have the Player roll an Animal Ken check, needing two (2) successes. If they succeed, the animals remain wary, but hesitate. If the Player only rolls one (trained) success, the hounds begin barking. The fuss will call the hound-master over, who will arrive at some inconvenient time for the Players. If he spots them before the Players have dealt with the dogs, he will shout an alarm and order the beasts to attack. Finally, if an untrained Player attempts and fails, or a trained Character fails by more than one, the dogs raise all kinds of hell, baying and snarling, and rise up to attack the offending Player, all thoughts of food cast aside.

Similarly, if the Players do not come with food (looking for the baggage train with the intent to fight their way to their gear), the hounds will respond accordingly. Again, the hound-master will arrive in due course and call guards down on the Players. In all likelihood - under geared and overwhelmed - the Players will probably die. Do your best to avoid having this happen by playing up the danger of the situation - a successful one-shot should be fun, and invite your Players back to the table to try out an actual campaign. That said, if you're playing with people who have gotten themselves into this situation, maybe killing them is the right answer.

Finally, if the Players are able to get the food to the hounds (either by feeding them directly, or by throwing the meat to them), the encounter plays out depending on the success of their survivalist. If the Players successfully found the Valerian, the hounds fall into a deep sleep relatively quickly. The large hounds succumbs as well, eventually, but takes longer than the rest of their pack. Should the Player's feed the hounds the Devil's Ire, however, the result is slightly different. Instead of falling into peaceful slumber, they go completely mad, baying loudly and causing hell. As the poison begins to work through their systems, they will run through the camp, knocking over anything in their path, and unable to distinguish friend from foe.

Should this happen, all pretense of stealth will be utterly destroyed, however, it might be of benefit to the Players, since the ensuing chaos will provide a perfect distraction for them to retrieve their gear from the baggage-train, though maybe not provide them with enough time to equip everything.

Approaching the Wagon

You approach the wagon, ready to take back what the Church Knights had stolen from you. Rounding the back, you nearly run into a cauldron large enough for a person to stand in. Embossed figures dance and writhe along its dull iron casing, and ancient letters crawl like serpents around the mouth of the great crucible.

A moment later, Hedeg shuffles into view, his hair swirling in the winter winds. 'Gods,' he whispers, holding a hand out to steady himself on the back of the wagon, 'it's worse than I thought.' The old man is facing the cauldron, and his voice, though low, is filled with loathing; his sightless eyes narrowed in utter revulsion.

'Quickly then,' he says after a moment, 'mustn't stay too close to this blasted thing,' and he moves off, letting the rest of you past.

Getting the Goods

The wagon is, of course, locked. Characters can attempt to unlock it using Hedeg's makeshift tools, but need two successes to open it, or, if they chose to work with Cob and he gave them stolen key, can open it automatically. Provided they still have time before they are found, Players can begin equipping their Characters - pulling on a single piece of armour costs on action, with a pair of gauntlets or greaves counting as one piece. Shields, most weapons, and helmets are considered free so long as Players mention this action. Otherwise, it's assumed that, while weapons are grabbed, Characters may have had other things on their minds instead of donning helms or strapping on shields.

If an alarm hasn't been raised, Players have three actions each before they are discovered, otherwise, they have a single action each. Alternatively, a limelight round can be used to don two pieces of equipment provided the Character is not engaged in combat.

The Iron Cauldron

Players will now, no doubt, be curious about the Iron Cauldron, and its ancient letters. A particularly enterprising Character might be tempted to read the script that appears on the relic, despite Hedeg's clear abhorrence for the artifact. If an attempt is made, have the Player roll for Decipher. Anything less than three (3) successes reveals nothing other than the script is clearly archaic, but nothing more.

Rolling four or greater, however, higher than this tells the Player that the language could be Naivunnan, a corrupted civilization, long lost to this world. Naivunnan texts are largely unknown, and other than a scrap ofhistory, they can't tell much more beyond this. In either case, studying the script is difficult, as it wavers sickeningly within the viewer's sight, and Hedeg will most likely scold the curious Player, saying that wiser minds know when to leave well enough alone.

Touching the chill metal will leech heat from a Character - indeed, it seems that the winter is far harsher near the cauldron, its bitter vileness bleeding into the marrow of any who try to stand close for too long. Climbing inside would be akin to dropping oneself into a bath of nitrogen, that is to say, a very poor choice, and probably fatal.

Once the Players have re-equipped themselves, continue on to the next section of the one-shot here.
