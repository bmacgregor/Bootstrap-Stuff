Like Rats in a Trap

You awaken, sore and groggy, to the sound of plodding horse-hooves and the clink of iron around your wrist. The realization that you’ve been captured blooms quickly and settles into your spine, where it mixes with the cold winter air and the dank gloom of the covered wagon’s interior. As your eyes adjust, it appears that you are not alone inside the cart.

In all, there are [the number of players, plus one] of you, and as you peer deeper into the confines of your moving cell, you are able make out a single door at the far end of the wagon-bed.

Some Questions

It’s too dark to clearly see everyone, but start thinking about what you look like. If you’re a warrior, are your hands calloused? Has your skin been weathered by the sun? Are you part of the Queen's Own, a mercenary, a wanderer? If you’re a mage, similar questions apply – do you smell of acrid herbs and smoke? Are you a hedge-wizard? A sorcerer’s apprentice who has run off, perhaps? A caution, however, should you choose this path - sorcery is highly illegal in Fornholm, the country you're currently in, and as such, perhaps your arcane skills might better be kept hidden for the moment.

This is the kind of game where who you are matters – in fact, that’s largely what drives the story. Your old grudges, dreams, and passions are the things that Blade is centered around, so when you choose something for your character, don’t do so lightly.

What you know right now is this: you are in the country of Fornholm, which is in the midst of a bloody civil war, of which there are several different sides. The old king is dead, and his eldest son was probably murdered by his loyal Dukes; his death made to look like a hunting accident. The remaining heir to the throne is suspected of poisoning her father, or at least being incompetent enough to send his healers and physikers away when he needed them most, though many suspect that it was the healers themselves that killed the king, and the princess didn't catch on until much too late.

Her half-brother - an illegitimate bastard who was given a military post and swept under the rug - is away, campaigning in distant lands. He probably hasn't heard of his father's death yet, or of the accusations of murder and treason that surely follow. Several other factions have sprouted up; namely that of the Council of Lords and those that fight under the banner of the True Queen, though new names are thrown into the ring almost daily to claim control of the rudderless country.

Of these factions, you've been taken by the Gray Church and its Witchfinders, a fanatical group led by the mad and powerful Mother Sarevin Caesless, head of the Church, and keen to add to her sphere of influence. The Gray Church is yet another faction locked in the struggle for Fornholm and the glittering jewel in her centre, the Ivory City of Marris, and among other things, it's known for the fervent hunt for those suspected of having the ungodly taint of magic in their veins.

What the zealous followers of the Church do with those unlucky enough to be caught is widely speculated, but escapes from the Chapel of Verity - the Church's seat of power - are all but unknown.

A bent figure near the door turns to regard each of you. From the moonlight streaming through the bars, you can almost see his face through the murk. He is an old man, bowed by great age, but his gaze is heavy, as if by simply looking he might drag your secrets screaming into the wagon to swirl amidst the frozen air like unwelcome ghosts.

You shudder, and his lips part in a rictus of a smile that lists insanely off to one side. He coughs wetly, and after a moment you realize that the old man is laughing into the night as he throws his head back like a wild animal, and shakes so hard that you're sure he'll fall into a seizure, or some kind of mad fit.

'Well met,' he crows. 'Well met indeed, though it seems rather bleak at the moment.' He pauses and cants his head to one side, as if trying to remember something important. 'I am called Hedeg,' he says. 'Hedeg of Embarl, and I have been looking forward to meeting you all for some time.'

A Narrative Beat

By now, the players will more than likely want to do things (jumpy lot that players tend to be). Too much narration tends to make their eyes glaze over, and slows down the action. So! Let them flex their RP muscles - now is, after all, the time for talk. Stress that - unless they have very keen eyesight - the interior of the wagon is almost pitch black. What's more, it's cold. This might not bother someone from the Winter Wastes, but any city-folk in the group will be decidedly uncomfortable in the chilly, cramped quarters.

They've been stripped of their gear, so bashing their way out of the wagon isn't likely. Should they try such an approach, be sure to mention that the wagon hasn't stopped moving yet, and that if by some chance the driver doesn't notice (all call a few guards over), then surely one of the accompanying horsemen will. Hedeg will watch escape attempts bemusedly, but isn't likely to participate - the old seer knows more than he lets on.

If they decide to talk to Hedeg, he will reveal a few things:

  • He knows how to get out of the wagon, but isn't interested in wandering around outside, alone in the cold, and won't risk his knees leaping to the road while the damned thing is moving
  • He saw each of the players be captured by the Church Knights. Luckily, their captor's do not include any Witchfinders - he spits after saying the name - but he thinks it's only a matter of time before they meet up with a larger contingent of 'crazy, raving lunatics'
  • He has a good idea of where everyone's equipment is being kept, but unless the player's help him escape, he'll never tell them how to slip past the hounds that patrol the camp when a halt is called for the night
Under no circumstances will Hedeg reveal any sort of magical ability (at least, he won't come out any say anything about it). Instead, he'll grin a gap-toothed smile, and laugh heartily; he's not one to tip his hand so early, and although he has plans for the players, he need to make sure they'll play the parts he has in mind for them.

Once the players agree to help Hedeg to safety - or at least as far as the forests' tree-line, read the next part:

The old man grins, "A bargain then!" he exclaims, his blind eyes crinkling at their edges. His hands slap together, and he capers around the small cell in a bizarre dance. He chortles crazily and the rhythm of his claps fall into a strange cadence that echoes throughout the cramped interior of the wagon. The sound amplifies, twisting in the air as it spirals out, into the night. In moments, it's all around you, a steady crashing, like a great bellows, or a smith working a piece of metal, and as it tears at your ears, you can hear Hedeg's gleeful, wailing laughter.

In the distance, horses scream as something enormous falls to the earth - the force of its crash causing the wagon to nearly tip onto its side. Only the driver's quick reflexes and mastery over his animals keep it upright. Hedeg stops his dance and looks around the room, triumphantly.

'Now then', he says, reaching a gnarled hand into his wispy, uneven hair, 'which one of you is good with locks?' And he tugs free a small pick and tension wrench hidden in his bedraggled mop, holding the tools blindly out to any of you that might take them.

Picking the Lock

Picking the wagon's lock would normally be a task of easy difficulty, however, because it must be done by reaching through the grill of the door, and the player doing the picking doesn't want to be noticed, it instead requires two (2) successes.

Note: This check is here mostly to get someone rolling dice. Picking the lock has to happen so that the players can escape the wagon, and isn't intended to act as a barrier to the players - in other words, even though it's a check they roll for, they can't really fail this one. It's here mainly for cinematic effect, and also because the door needs to 'feel like' it's locked. This check will also take care of the shackles around each prisoner's wrists, though it seems that Hedeg either was left unbound, or had slipped free earlier.
