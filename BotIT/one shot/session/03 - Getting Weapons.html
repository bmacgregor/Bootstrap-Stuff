The Hounds

You find the baggage-train at the rear of the convoy - a large wagon, not unlike the one you and your new companions recently escaped, though probably better kept. A pack of hound's lounge around it, apparently not bothered by the snows. From your vantage point, it looks like there are five of the beasts. Four of them are unremarkable; the average war-dog one might expect to be set on peasants, each well muscled and vicious, but the fifth is massive. A great, shaggy beast that towers over the rest, though now rests by one of the wagon's wheels.

It's up to the players on how to proceed. If they have the Valerian (or what they think is the Valerian), now would be the time to mix it with the raw meat stolen from the hound master's saddlebags and throw it - hopefully from somewhere out of view - to the dogs.

If a player comes within view, so long as they're holding food, the dogs won't immediately attack - low growls will be heard, and the large hound will turn its massive head to look at the player. Feeding the dogs this way is extremely ill-advised. Have the player roll an Animal Ken check, needing two (2) successes. If they succeed, the animals remain wary, but hesitate. If the player fails, the hounds begin barking. The fuss will call the hound-master over, who will arrive at some inconvenient time for the players. If he spots them before the players have dealt with the dogs, he will shout an alarm and order the hounds to attack. Finally, if the player botches this roll, the dogs raise all kinds of hell, baying and snarling, and rise up to attack the offending player, all thoughts of food cast aside.

Similarly, if the players do not come with food (looking for the baggage train with the intent to fight their way to their gear), the hounds will respond accordingly. Again, the hound-master will arrive in due course and call guards down on the players. In all likelihood - under geared and overwhelmed - the players will probably die. Do your best to avoid having this happen by playing up the danger of the situation - a successful one-shot should be fun, and invite your players back to the table to try out an actual campaign. That said, if you're playing with people who have gotten themselves into this situation, maybe killing them is the right answer?

Finally, if the players are able to get the food to the hounds (either by feeding them directly, or by throwing the meat to them), the encounter plays out depending on the success of their survivalist. If the players successfully found the Valerian, the hounds fall into a deep sleep relatively quickly. The large hound succumbs too, eventually, but takes longer than the rest of his pack. Should the player's feed the hounds the Devil's Ire, however, the result is slightly different. Instead of falling into peaceful slumber, they go completely mad, baying loudly and causing hell. As the poison begins to work through their systems, they will run through the camp, knocking over anything in their path, and unable to distinguish friend from foe.

Should this happen, all pretense of stealth will be utterly destroyed, however, it might be of benefit to the players, since the ensuing chaos will provide a perfect distraction for them to retrieve their gear from the baggage-train.

Approaching the Wagon

Having dealt with the hounds, you approach the wagon, ready to takes back what the Church Knights had stolen from you. Rounding the back, you nearly run into a cauldron large enough for a person to stand in. Embossed figures dance and writhe along its dull iron casing, and ancient letters crawl like serpents around the mouth of the great crucible.

A moment later, Hedeg shuffles into view, his hair swirling in the winter winds. 'Gods,' he whispers, holding a hand out to steady himself on the back of the wagon, 'it's worse than I thought.' The old man is facing the cauldron, and his voice, though low, is filled with loathing; his sightless eyes narrowed in utter revulsion.

'Quickly then,' he says after a moment, 'mustn't stay too close to this blasted thing,' and he moves off, letting the rest of you past to claim your missing gear.

The Iron Cauldron

Players will now, no doubt, be curious about the Iron Cauldron, and its ancient letters. A particularly enterprising character might be tempted to read the script that appears on the relic, despite Hedeg's clear abhorrence for the artifact. If an attempt is made, have the player roll for Decipher. Anything less than three (3) successes reveals nothing other than the script is clearly archaic, but nothing more.

Rolling higher than this tells the player that the language could be Naivunnan, a corrupted civilization, long lost to this world. Naivunnan texts are largely unknown, and other than a scrap of history, they can't tell much more beyond this. In either case, studying the script is difficult, as it wavers sickeningly within the viewer's sight, and Hedeg will most likely scold the curious player, saying that wiser minds than the players know when to leave well enough alone.

Touching the chill metal will leech heat from a player - indeed, it seems that the winter is far harsher near the cauldron, its bitter vileness bleeding into the marrow of any who try to stand close for too long. Climbing inside would be akin to dropping oneself into a bath of nitrogen, that is to say, deadly.
