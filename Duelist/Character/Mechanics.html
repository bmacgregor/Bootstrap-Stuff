Game Mechanics

This section covers the types of dice used in Duelist, describes the six primary Attributes that each Character has, goes over how Players roll for Checks (things they want their Characters to do), and talks about the Difficulty of those Checks.

Dice Used

Duelist uses ten-sided dice (or d10's) for nearly everything in the game. Players will need to have at least ten d10's available to them, and in some situations (like combat, for instance) they might need even more. Players will also need two six-sided dice (d6's). Ideally, one d6 should be red and the other, black, but the colours don't really matter. What is important is that a Player has two d6's, and that they are different colours. This is gone into more detail in the Combat chapter, but to briefly explain:

When combat starts, Players have to decide if they want to attack or defend. Unless its obvious (like in an ambush, or on a charge), opponents will have to gauge one another, trying to figure out when an opportune time to attack might be. To represent this, opponents will conceal one of the d6's in their hand, hiding the other one away.

A red d6 indicates an attack, while a black d6 means the Character is defending. The dice are revealed at the same time, and might result in the opponents attacking each other at the same time, only one attacking, or the combatants circling and waiting for an opening. This is more fully explained in the Combat chapter, however, and is included here as a note for the curious.

Attributes

Attributes in Duelist are split into two broad categories, Mind and Body, with each describing a measure of a Character's strength, dexterity, and toughness.


Intellect

Intellect is a measure of a Character's strength of mind. It determines how quickly a Character can learn things, their general knowledge, and the amount of power they can throw into their spells. A Character with high intellect can put massive force behind their sorceries.

Brawn

Brawn describes a Character's raw, physical power. With high strength, a Character can deliver crippling strikes and batter down doors, as well as how much a Character can lift at a given time, and a general idea of how much brute force they can bring to bear.

Cunning

Cunning represents a Character's ability to out-think opponents. It is as much used by generals as it is by thieves, and is used for outmaneuvering in battles, trap-making and trap-finding, and creating devious puzzles. Combined with sorceries, Cunning can be used to find gaps in an opponents defence.

Agility

Agility determines how easily a Character can avoid punishment; it governs dodging in combat, how easily a Character is able to evade killing blows, and their speed. A truly agile Character can move an incredible speed, and often uses blitz-like tactics to deal with opponents.

Will

Will is how strong a Character's mind is. For sorceries, it governs how long a Duelist can hold a spell, and to what extent they are able to increase the effects of their sorceries. A Character that can envelop a city in a protective shield or bring time to a crawl possesses an incredibly strong Will.

Endurance

Endurance determines a Character's physical mettle - it governs how long they're able to stay in Combat, how much gear they can use, and the amount of punishment they're able to take. A Duelist encased in heavy armour, carrying a tower shield, and wielding a warhammer has a high Endurance.

Attributes are rated from one to eight, with a score of one signifying a crippling impairment in a given area, while an eight denotes complete mastery of the associated score.

1 Severe Disability
2 Incapable
3 Mediocre
4 Average

The Need for Checks

Generally, Checks don't need to be rolled. Duelists are competent adventurers, and games shouldn't be bogged down by rolling for every little thing - menial tasks like finding wood for a campfire can be hand-waved rather than checking if a Character has the necessary survival skills and then rolling dice to see if they're able to find wood.

Things that are more difficult, such as forging weapons or pushing a horse to gallop across broken country can generally skirt the need for rolling a Check as well, provided that they are role-played. This adds flavour to a game and the experience of everyone at the table; for example, if a Player needed to scale some cliffs under the cover of nightfall, they might role-play it out, describing the lashing winds and salt-filled gusts from the turbulent sea below as they grope blindly for handholds, hauling themselves toward the clifftop.

Although there is an element of danger here, it's assumed that the Game Master has included this scene for its cinematic nature and to deepen the story for the Players. As such, it's assumed that the Players will survive the climb so that they can carry on with the adventure. Allowing Players to role-play difficult sections out, rather than rolling for them moves the story along and also immerses the table in the world.

When Should Checks be Rolled?

If a Check has a significant element of failure, such as the death of several NPC's, derailment of a Character's plans, Character death, or any possible branch in the Game Masters story that is directly affected by the Character's success or failure, a Check is rolled. Checks should serve as junctures in the plot - a time when something can go as planned or spiral out of control.

How to Roll for a Check

First, the Game Master chooses a Difficulty Level (see below). This is based on how hard or easy the proposed action is - something like vaulting over a table in a melee is considered to be fairly simple, while tracking someone through a swamp under heavy rains a week late is next to impossible*. It's considered good form to tell the Player what the difficulty level is before they roll the dice.

The Difficulty Level is how many successes a Player needs to roll to succeed at the Check. A success is any roll of six (6) or higher on a d10. Anything lower than this (five or less) counts as a failure. The number of successes a Player rolls is called the Margin of Success, and indicates how well a Character does at any given Check.

Next, the Player rolls dice based on the skill being tested. If a Character doesn't have the relevant skill, they can still make an attempt, but require twice as many successes as required by the Check. For instance, if a Character needs three successes to calm an enraged bear, they could either use an appropriate skill to try and get the three successes needed, or they could use an attribute. If the Player opts to use an attribute, they would need to roll six (3 * 2 = 6) successes to calm the bear.

* A Note to Game Masters: unless you have a good reason, don't make things impossible. That said, Players shouldn't be able to Mary Sue their way through everything, citing this small note as reason for why "befriending the dragon / seducing the main villain / creating their own demi-plane" ought to be possible. Use common sense.


Opposed Checks

NPC's can oppose a Character's Check. This can happen in a variety of situations; some examples include when a Character tries to lie to someone, attempts to outrun (or catch) an NPC, and in most combat situations. In these cases, where one party is opposing another, a Check is rolled, as normal, but there isn't a Difficulty Level assigned. Instead, the Character's successes are compared against the NPC's, and whomever has the most is the successful party. In the event that a Margin of Success is needed (for calculating damage, for instance) the higher amount of successes is subtracted from the lower total.

Example: Rell is trying to barter with a shopkeep to get a better price for the wolf pelt she's selling. Because the shopkeep is unwilling to give her a better deal (and loves to barter), the Game Master calls for an Opposed Negotiation Check. Rell's Negotiation is a four (having dealt with frugal merchants for years), and the shopkeep has a three. Both roll their respective pool of dice.

Rell rolls a 9, 4, 7 and a 1. The 9 and the 7 are counted as successes (both 6 or greater) for a total of two successes. The shopkeep rolls a 10, 5, and a 3 for a single success.

Rell's total (2) is subtracted from the shopkeep's total (1), leaving her with a Margin of Success of 1. Somewhat grudgingly, the shopkeep gives her a slightly better price for the pelt, his eyes twinkling from the exchange.

In the above example, Rell's Margin of Success directly impacted the price difference for her sale. Had she rolled more successes, the Game Master would have been obligated to give her an even better price. Conversely, had the shopkeep beaten her in the Check, he could have offered her an even lower price.

Once a Check is made, it stands. Further Checks can be made, but are impacted by the previous attempts. For instance, if Rell wanted to drive an even harder bargain, she could try to Negotiate again, however, her successes would only be half as effective. This means that she would need two successes for every one of the shopkeep. Characters can certainly push their luck with this (re-checking for traps, further trying to intimidate a foe, etc.) but things become much harder if follow-up Checks are attempted. In Rell's case, although she's secured a better price for her pelt, she could risk offending the shopkeep, being thrown out of the store, or outright banned from coming back.

Timed Checks

Timed Checks are handled a little differently than an Opposed Check. To succeed at a timed task, the Game Master will assign a Difficulty Total and a number of attempts the Character has to reach the total. For instance, Characters desperately searching a library for a scroll to give them information needed to stop a demon lord from being summoned might need to accumulate twenty-five Search successes across three rounds of Searching (a round being long enough for each Player to roll once). If they are successful, the scroll is found in time. If they are too slow, however, a hellish portal will tear the fabric of reality, allowing the Demon Lord and its subjects to pour through into their world...

Tied Checks

A tied Check results in a success for the party that wants to maintain the status quo. In combat, this means an attacker misses (the status quo being that the defender is uninjured). If a Character is attempting to steal something from an NPC, it indicates that the NPC keeps the object (though, doesn't notice the Character's attempt, this also being part of the status quo). In Rell's example, it would mean that the shopkeep is firm on the price offered.

Failed Checks

While a successful Check has a positive Margin of Success, a failed Check has a Margin of Failure. Generally speaking, a small Margin of Failure simply means that the Check fails. A Character is unable to climb a wall in time, and might be caught by the guards; an attempt at thievery is unsuccessful, and might be noticed - in short, something goes awry.

If a Check is failed by two or more, a minor complication ensues, as determined by the Game Master. This won't be anything catastrophic, but will definitely put a Character in a difficult spot. A Character Navigating their way through the trackless wilderness might blunder around, arriving late to their destination, or unable to find a suitable spot to camp for the night and have to make due sleeping in an exposed place. Someone attempting to translate a piece of lore might believe the text says something entirely different, and while this might not cause massive detriment to their studies, it will certainly cause an issue which will leave the Player on the back-foot.

In short, a Margin of Failure by two or more is a setback, something that wastes time and resources, but that can (with effort) be corrected. In the case of a thief attempting to filch a purse, their attempt will be noticed, but they might be able to correct their blunder without a significant amount of trouble.

A Margin of Failure of five or more, however, implies dire consequences. In a chase, a Character might run headlong into a door that opened at the exact wrong time, sending them sprawling. They will likely have to contend with whomever opened the door in the first place (as they likely pelted right into the unlucky person, and based on the nature of the failure, this is probably a guard, or several guards) and also deal with their pursuers who - if they haven't caught up yet - have certainly gained on them due to the mishap. Depending on the pursuers, this likely means a fight which the Character might be woefully unprepared for.

Difficulty Levels

Difficulty Levels are directly related to how hard any given task is. The table below offers target Difficulty Levels, a description of how difficult a given task might be, and an example of a similar Check.

Level Difficulty Example
1 Easy Wading through waist high swamps; disguising a shallow scar with make-up
2 Moderate Remaining concealed in the shadows; Deciphering a known language
3 Challenging Riding at a full gallop through a thick forest; trailing someone through a busy marketplace
4 Demanding Spotting week-old tracks; Balancing on an unstable shred of timber in the wind
5 Extreme Forging a Royal writ, including signature and seal; Climbing a sheer overhang
6 + Nearly Impossible Arcing an arrow over a high battlement to hit a specific person; Noticing an invisible creature

Difficulty Totals

Difficulty Totals are used exclusively in Timed Checks, and are presented similarly to the Difficulty Level table. To create a Timed Check, multiply the number of Players in the party by the number of successes per round that the Check will require. Finally, multiple this number by the number of rounds given for the timed Check.

Difficulty Multiplier
Easy 0.25
Moderate 0.5
Challenging 1
Demanding 2
Extreme 3
Nearly Impossible 4

Example: For his game, Mike wants to incorporate a timed Check. He has four Players in his party, and wants the Check to be done quickly - two rounds. Multiplying the Players by the time he gets a total of eight (4 players x 2 rounds = 8). Mike wants this Check to be Demanding for his Players; referencing the Timed Check total, he finds that Demanding Checks are multiplied by 2. He multiplies these numbers together to get a Demanding Timed Check Total of 16 - or 16 successes required in two rounds.


Bear in mind that penalties for using an Attribute during a Timed Skill Check still apply, requiring double the number of successes.
